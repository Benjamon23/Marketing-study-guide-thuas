Week 01 · Foundations

Introduction to Advanced Marketing & Sales

The opening week sets the vocabulary for everything after it. The one idea that ties the whole course together is the marketing mix (the 4 Ps). Each later week is really a deep dive into one P. If you hold that map in your head, the other 12 weeks stop feeling like 13 separate topics and start feeling like one system.

1 Marketing vs Sales

They are not the same job. Marketing creates demand and builds the relationship over time (research, segmentation, branding, promotion). Sales converts that demand into a transaction (the actual deal, the close, the account). Marketing is one-to-many and long-term; sales is one-to-one and immediate.

2 The marketing concept

The modern view: you win by understanding customer needs better than rivals and delivering value, not by just pushing product. This evolves into societal marketing (balance customer wants, company profit, and society's wellbeing) which comes back hard in Week 4.

Production concept Product concept Selling concept Marketing concept Societal concept

3 The 4 Ps — the spine of the course

The marketing mix is the set of controllable levers a company pulls to position its offer. Services stretch it to 7 Ps (adding People, Process, Physical evidence). Notice how four of your exam weeks are literally one P each.

The 4 Ps mapped to your course weeks
Know the difference Marketing vs Sales
MarketingCreates and grows demand. One-to-many, long-term, owns research, brand, promotion.
SalesCaptures demand. One-to-one, short-term, owns the deal, the close, the account relationship.

  • "Societal marketing concept" is the answer whenever a case mentions balancing profit with society or the environment (see Emirates Q14, sustainability).

Week 02 · Emirates case territory

Market Understanding & Research

Before you can market anything you have to read the room: the customer, the company, the competitors, the wider world. This week is a toolkit of analysis frameworks. The exam loves asking "which research or analysis tool fits this situation?", so the goal is to know exactly what each tool looks at and when you reach for it.

1 What market research is

Kotler's definition: systematic problem analysis, model building and fact-finding to improve marketing decisions. The point is reducing risk before you commit money. Research splits by what you study:

  • Consumer research — customers, ex-customers, non-customers, influencers.
  • Company research — your own product, price, promotion, placement, service.
  • Competitor research — rivals' strengths, weaknesses, strategies.
  • Industry research — trends, growth, market developments.

2 The 5-step research process

If a question asks about the order of research, this is it. You define before you collect, and you collect before you analyse.

Market research process

3 Internal vs external, micro vs macro

Split the world into what you control and what you don't. Internal = inside the company. External splits again into the microenvironment (close forces you can influence: customers, suppliers, distributors, competitors) and the macroenvironment (big forces you only react to: the PESTEL factors).

4 PESTEL — scanning the macro environment

Six external forces no single company controls. Your deck called it PESTE but the mock answer key uses PESTLE / PESTEL (it adds Legal). Learn all six so either label is safe.

PoliticalEconomicSocial TechnologicalEnvironmentalLegal

5 Porter's Five Forces — is this industry attractive?

Not about one rival, about the structure of the whole industry and how much profit is possible in it. Five forces squeeze the central rivalry.

Porter's Five Forces

6 SWOT — your position in one grid

The classic 2x2. The trick that trips people: Strengths and Weaknesses are internal (about you), Opportunities and Threats are external (about the world). Emirates' fuel-efficient fleet is a strength; new air-travel regulation is a threat.

SWOT — internal vs external, helpful vs harmful

7 Qualitative vs quantitative, and the value proposition canvas

  • Qualitative = the "why". Opinions, motivations, focus groups, interviews. Small samples, deep.
  • Quantitative = the "how many". Numbers, surveys, statistics. Large samples, measurable.
  • Value Proposition Canvas matches your offer to the customer's jobs, pains and gains. It is the bridge between research and what you actually build.
Know the difference Which tool looks at what
SWOTYour own position. Internal (S/W) + external (O/T) in one grid.
PESTELOnly the macro, external, uncontrollable forces.
Porter's Five ForcesIndustry attractiveness and profit potential, structural.
Micro vs MacroMicro = close forces you can influence. Macro = big forces you only react to.
Exam notes Week 2 traps
  • PESTE vs PESTEL: the exam key uses PESTLE/PESTEL (with Legal). If you see "external factors affecting Harley in Europe", the answer is PESTLE, not SWOT, because the question says external only.
  • SWOT vs PESTEL trap: SWOT is the catch-all (internal + external). PESTEL is external only. If the question restricts to "external factors", pick PESTEL.
  • Buyer Persona research is the answer when a case wants to understand a target audience before a campaign (Emirates Q8), not NPS or Porter's.
  • Regulation, interest rates, sustainability rules = macro factors. A new competitor entering = micro (Porter), not macro.

Recall trick Week 2 anchors
  • Research order: Define · Design · Collect · Analyse · Report.
  • SWOT is a mirror, PESTEL is a telescope. SWOT looks at you (plus the world), PESTEL only at the far horizon.
  • Porter's = New entrants · Substitutes · Buyers · Suppliers · Rivalry. Four forces push on the rivalry in the middle.
  • S and W live inside the company. O and T live outside.
Week 03 · Emirates case territory

Customer Persona & Customer Journey

You can't serve everyone, so you choose who to serve (STP), build a clear picture of that person (persona), then map how they move from "never heard of you" to "tells their friends about you" (the journey). This week is the heart of the Emirates case and shows up constantly.

1 STP: Segmentation, Targeting, Positioning

The core strategy sequence. You cut the market into groups, pick the groups worth serving, then plant a clear idea of your brand in their heads.

  • Segmentation — divide the market into groups with similar needs.
  • Targeting — choose which segment(s) to actually serve.
  • Positioning — design a distinct place in the customer's mind versus rivals.
The STP funnel: broad market to sharp position

2 The 4 segmentation bases

Four lenses for cutting a market. A good case answer often names the right base.

  • Demographic — age, gender, income, education, family size.
  • Geographic — country, city, climate, density.
  • Psychographic — lifestyle, values, interests, personality.
  • Behavioral — usage, loyalty, benefits sought, journey stage.

A segment is only worth it if it passes five checks: Measurable, Accessible, Substantial, Differentiable, Actionable.

3 Value proposition strategies

How much benefit you give versus what you charge. Five are winners, the rest lose. Emirates runs More for More: more luxury, higher price.

Value proposition matrix (benefits vs price)

4 The customer journey

The path a customer travels with your brand. Each stage needs different marketing. Note the goal shifts from getting noticed early to turning fans into promoters at the end.

Customer journey stages

SMM vs SMOSMM = marketing through social. SMO = optimizing your profiles/posts for visibility.

  • Segmentation vs Targeting vs Positioning: dividing = segmentation, choosing = targeting, shaping the mental image = positioning. Questions test which step a described action belongs to.

  • "Discover More" showing Indian landmarks to get noticed = the Awareness stage (Harley Q9).
  • SEO is free and slow, SEA is paid and instant. If the case says "improve organic rankings" it's SEO; "paid search ads" it's SEA.

  • Segmentation bases: Demo · Geo · Psycho · Behavioral (who they are, where they are, what they believe, what they do).
  • Journey: Awareness → Consideration → Purchase → Retention → Advocacy. Stranger to fan.
  • Good segment = MASDA: Measurable, Accessible, Substantial, Differentiable, Actionable.
Week 04 · Emirates case territory

Branding & Purposefulness

A brand is the gap between what a product costs to make and what people will happily pay for it. This week is about building that gap (brand equity) and the modern twist: customers now expect brands to stand for something beyond profit. The sustainability angle is exactly why Emirates Q14 exists.

1 Brand basics and identity

  • Brand — the name, symbol, design that identifies and separates a seller.
  • Branding — the ongoing process of creating and managing that brand.
  • Brand identity — what the company sends out: visual identity (logo, colours, type) + verbal identity (tone, messaging).
  • Brand image — what the customer actually perceives. Identity is the sender's intent, image is the receiver's reality.

2 Positioning strategies

Five ways to claim a spot in the customer's mind.

Price (premium vs value) Characteristics (reliable, safe, fast) Use (when/how it's used) Quality (luxury, superior) Competition (vs a rival)

3 Brand equity, built bottom-up

Brand equity is the value the brand adds through perception. It is built in layers: first they recognise you, then they associate things with you, then they trust your quality, and finally they stay loyal.

Brand equity pyramid (built bottom to top)

4 Purposeful, societal and sustainable marketing

The modern expectation: marketing that considers people, society and planet, not only sales.

  • Purposeful marketing — marketing guided by a mission beyond profit.
  • Societal marketing — balance three things at once: company profit, customer wants, and society's wellbeing.
  • Sustainable marketing — meet today's needs without wrecking future generations' ability to meet theirs.
  • Shared value — the idea that solving social problems can itself create business opportunities.
Know the difference The two that get confused
Brand identityWhat the company sends (intended). You design it.
Brand imageWhat the customer receives (perceived). They decide it.
Societal marketingBalance profit + customer + society, now.
Sustainable marketingProtect future generations' needs too.
Exam notes Week 4 traps
  • Sustainability + long-term societal interest balanced with customer satisfaction = societal marketing concept (Emirates Q14). It is the one that says "balance" of profit, customer and society.
  • Premium pricing "justified by the value of luxury services" links to value-based positioning, not cost. The brand equity is what lets them charge more.
  • If the question is about what the company intends to project, that's identity. If it's about how customers actually see them, that's image.

Week 05 · Emirates + Harley territory

Internationalization

Going abroad is a series of choices: which products to back (BCG), which growth direction to take (Ansoff), how to enter a country (entry modes), and how much to adapt versus standardize (glocalization). The Harley-in-India and Emirates-in-Thailand questions all live here.

1 Internationalization vs Globalization

  • Internationalization — doing business across several countries, often within a region. Step by step.
  • Globalization — developing, producing and selling worldwide as one connected operation.

2 BCG Matrix: which products to fund

Plots your products on market growth vs market share. Tells you where to invest, milk, or cut.

BCG Matrix (growth vs share)

3 Ansoff Matrix: which growth direction

Four ways to grow, by combining existing/new products with existing/new markets. This was tested directly in the Harley case (the "Discover More" India campaign = Market Development: existing product, new market).

Ansoff Matrix (products vs markets)

4 Market entry modes

How you actually set foot in a foreign market. They trade off in a clear pattern: the more control you want, the more risk and commitment you take, and the less flexibility you keep.

Entry modes: low to high commitment, control and risk

5 Glocalization: standardize or adapt

"Think global, act local." You decide how much of the marketing mix stays the same everywhere (cheaper, consistent) versus tailored to each country (relevant, costly).

  • Standardization — same strategy across markets. Lower cost, economies of scale.
  • Adaptation — modify to local conditions. More relevant, more expensive.
  • Psychic distance — the perceived gap between countries (language, culture, business norms) that makes a market harder to enter.
  • Incremental entry (start with similar countries) vs leapfrogging (jump straight to distant markets).
Know the difference BCG vs Ansoff
BCG MatrixLooks at your current product portfolio: growth vs share. Decides invest / milk / drop.
Ansoff MatrixLooks at growth direction: products vs markets. Decides how to expand.
StandardizationSame everywhere. Cheaper, consistent brand.
AdaptationTailored locally. Relevant, costlier.
Exam notes Week 5 traps
  • Existing product into a new country = Market Development (Harley "Discover More" in India, Q6). New product to existing customers = Product Development (LiveWire to current Harley fans).
  • Harley partnering with Hero MotoCorp to run Indian operations = a joint venture: shared risk and resources.
  • Local partnerships and codeshares for Emirates in Thailand = entry via partnership that gives local insight + streamlined distribution (Emirates Q5).
  • Entry mode logic: export = lowest risk/control, FDI = highest. If a case wants maximum control, it's FDI; minimum commitment, it's exporting.

  • Think global, act local = glocalization. Standardize to save, adapt to fit.
Week 06 · Harley + IKEA territory

Customer Experience (CX)

Experience is the whole feeling a customer carries from every interaction with your brand. This week is where the metrics live: NPS, CSAT, CES, CLV, churn. The exam loves asking "which metric measures this?", so know exactly what each one captures and its formula.

1 CX vs UX

The single most-confused pair in this whole course. CX is the whole relationship with the brand across every touchpoint. UX is narrower: the experience of using one product or system.

Customer Experience vs User Experience

2 The metrics (and what each one really measures)

  • NPS (Net Promoter Score) — loyalty: would you recommend us? Scale 0 to 10.
  • CSAT (Customer Satisfaction Score) — satisfaction with a specific product or interaction.
  • CES (Customer Effort Score) — how easy it was to get something done.
  • CLV (Customer Lifetime Value) — total value a customer brings over the whole relationship.
  • Churn rate — % of customers lost in a period. The opposite of retention.
NPS: how the 0–10 score splits
The formulas worth memorizing

3 Omnichannel, cross-sell, upsell

  • Omnichannel — one seamless, connected experience across all channels (online and offline blend together).
  • Multichannel — present on many channels, but they aren't joined up.
  • Cross-sell — sell a related product (fries with the burger).
  • Upsell — sell a higher-value version (large instead of medium).
Know the difference The metric that fits the question
NPSLoyalty / willingness to recommend.
CSATSatisfaction with a specific thing.
CESHow little effort it took.
CLVTotal value over the lifetime.
Cross vs UpCross = related item. Up = pricier version.
Exam notes Week 6 traps
  • The Harley Q1 trap: "measure customer satisfaction directly" with options NPS, CES, churn, CLV. CSAT isn't offered, so NPS is the best fit by elimination (the others measure effort, loss and value, not satisfaction). If CSAT is an option, pick CSAT.
  • "How easy was it" = CES. "Would you recommend" = NPS. Don't mix them.
  • IKEA Place giving one connected experience across app and store = omnichannel (IKEA Q1).
  • CLV is about total long-term value, never a single purchase.

Recall trick Week 6 anchors
  • CX is the marriage, UX is one date. CX = whole brand relationship, UX = using one product.
  • Metric cheat: NPS = loyalty, CSAT = satisfaction, CES = effort, CLV = value.
  • NPS = Promoters minus Detractors. Passives are invisible.
  • Cross = sideways (related), Up = upward (pricier).
Week 07 · IKEA + Harley territory

CRM & Lead Acquisition

CRM is how you manage relationships at scale, and lead acquisition is how you fill the top of the pipeline with future customers. This week pairs the three CRM types with the lead funnel and the inbound-versus-outbound split. The IKEA app questions about nurturing leads come straight from here.

1 What CRM is and why

Customer Relationship Management: the strategy and software for managing every interaction with current and potential customers. Goals are to retain customers, increase loyalty, personalise, and grow lifetime value. Common KPIs: CLV, NPS, churn, resolution time.

2 The three CRM types

This is a classic "which type is this?" exam item. Each does a different job.

Three CRM types

3 The lead funnel

A stranger becomes a customer in stages. The job of lead acquisition is to move people down this funnel and nurture them along the way.

From stranger to customer

4 Generating and acquiring leads

  • Lead generation — attracting interest and capturing contact details (the spark).
  • Lead acquisition — the fuller process of turning that interest into an actual acquired customer (gen + nurture + convert).
  • Lead nurturing — building the relationship with content and follow-up until they're ready to buy.
  • Lead magnet — the freebie (ebook, demo, discount) you trade for contact details.
  • CTA & landing page — the button and the focused page that capture the lead.
  • Social proof — testimonials, case studies, reviews, influencer/UGC that build trust.
Know the difference The pairs that get tested
Operational CRMAutomates and runs sales/marketing/service tasks.
Analytical CRMAnalyses data to find insight (churn, segments).
Collaborative CRMShares customer info across teams/partners.
Inbound vs OutboundInbound = they come to you (content, SEO). Outbound = you go to them (cold calls, ads).
Lead gen vs acquisitionGen = capture interest. Acquisition = the whole journey to becoming a customer.
Exam notes Week 7 traps
  • IKEA Place using AR to engage people early in the funnel = supporting lead acquisition / lead generation (IKEA Q9, Q12).
  • Using AR to "nurture leads into qualified prospects" = the nurturing step (IKEA Q12).
  • Harley using CRM to spot churn and personalise = analytical CRM feeding strategy (Harley Q14).
  • Content, SEO, a helpful blog that pulls people in = inbound. Cold calls, cold email, interruptive ads = outbound.

Week 08 · Reconstructed (no deck)

Proposition: Products & Services

Verify this No flashcards existed for this week

This week was missing from the Quizlet decks, so the content below is rebuilt from standard marketing theory that this course uses. The frameworks (product levels, service characteristics) are textbook-standard and very likely correct, but cross-check against your Week 8 slides before relying on it.

This week is the "Product" P of the mix. It answers: what are we actually offering, and what makes it worth buying? The two big frameworks are Kotler's three product levels (a product is more than the physical thing) and the four service characteristics (services behave differently from products).

Pricing

Price is the only P that brings in money. The rest cost money. This week is about how you set price (cost, value or competition based), and which launch strategy you use (skim high or penetrate low). The Harley LiveWire questions are pure skimming, so nail that distinction.

1 The three pricing approaches

Three ways to set a price

2 Skimming vs penetration (the launch decision)

When launching, you either start high and lower over time (skimming) or start low to grab share fast (penetration). Harley launches the LiveWire high for early adopters: that is skimming.

Skimming vs penetration over time

3 Price elasticity

  • Elastic demand — small price change causes a big change in quantity sold (price-sensitive, e.g. snacks).
  • Inelastic demand — price barely affects demand (essentials, addictions, must-haves like petrol or insulin).
  • Unit elastic — % change in price equals % change in quantity.

4 The product life cycle (price changes with the stage)

Pricing isn't set once. It shifts as a product ages through four stages.

Product life cycle

Place is about getting the product to the customer: through how many middlemen, how widely, and how directly. This week covers channel levels (how many intermediaries), distribution intensity (how many outlets), and the modern shift to selling direct.

1 Channels and intermediaries

  • Marketing channel — the set of organisations that move a product from producer to consumer.
  • Retailer — sells to final consumers.
  • Wholesaler — buys in bulk, sells to retailers/businesses.
  • Distributor — moves and often represents the product, sometimes exclusively.
  • Disintermediation — cutting out the middlemen (e.g. brand sells direct online).

2 Channel levels (direct vs indirect)

Count the intermediaries between maker and buyer. Zero = direct. One or more = indirect.

Channel levels

3 Distribution intensity

How many outlets carry your product. The choice signals your brand: ubiquitous and cheap, or rare and exclusive.

Distribution intensity

4 Modern channels and conflict

  • DTC (Direct-to-Consumer) — brand sells straight to buyers, no middlemen.
  • Multichannel / hybrid — using several channels together.
  • Vertical conflict — friction between different levels (producer vs retailer).
  • Horizontal conflict — friction between firms at the same level (two retailers of the same brand).

Intensive vs ExclusiveIntensive = everywhere. Selective = a few. Exclusive = one/very few.

The non-obvious stuff · read this carefully

⚡ Danger Zone — Key Differences

These are the pairs and concepts that look similar, sound similar, or seem obvious but aren't. This is exactly where marks get lost. Not definitions — the tricky distinctions the exam exploits.

Week 1 + 4 · Concepts

The marketing concept evolution trap

Common trap
Societal Marketing≠Sustainable Marketing
They sound like the same thing. They're not. Societal = balance three things right now: company profit, customer wants, and society's wellbeing. Sustainable = protecting future generations' ability to meet their needs. One is about the present balance, one is about the future. When a question says "long-term environmental impact" → sustainable. When it says "balance profit, customer and society" → societal.
Not obvious
Selling Concept≠Marketing Concept
The selling concept says: make the product first, then push it hard until people buy. The marketing concept flips it: understand what people need first, then make something for that. This seems obvious but the exam will describe a company's approach and ask you to classify it. The tell: if they're starting from the product and pushing → selling. If they're starting from research and need → marketing.
Week 2 · Research Tools

The "which analysis tool" traps

Common trap
SWOTvsPESTEL
Both include external factors. The trap: SWOT is broader (internal + external), so it seems like the "complete" answer. But when the question explicitly says "external factors only", the answer is always PESTEL. SWOT when they want the full picture. PESTEL when the word "external" appears alone.
Not obvious
Porter's Five Forces≠competitive analysis
Porter's is NOT about comparing yourself to specific rivals. It's about the structural attractiveness of the whole industry — how much profit potential exists in it. It answers "is this a good industry to be in?" not "how do we beat Company X?"
Not obvious
Micro environment≠internal
Micro = external but close forces you can influence: customers, suppliers, distributors, competitors. Students often think "micro" means "inside the company." It doesn't. Internal = employees, finances, culture. Micro = the nearby external world.
Week 3 · STP

Segmentation vs Targeting vs Positioning

Common trap
Segmenting≠Targeting
People use these interchangeably. They're two separate steps. Segmenting = dividing the whole market into groups. You haven't chosen anyone yet. Targeting = deciding which segment(s) to actually go after. Emirates segments the market into economy, business, first class travellers — that's segmentation. Choosing to invest in first-class luxury travellers — that's targeting.
Not obvious
PsychographicvsBehavioural
Psychographic = who they are inside: values, lifestyle, personality, self-image ("I see myself as free-spirited"). Behavioural = what they actually do: how often they buy, loyalty stage, benefits they want. Harley fans' "freedom" identity = psychographic. Frequency of purchases or loyalty tier = behavioural.
Not obvious
Positioning
Positioning is NOT a price tag or product feature. It's a place in the customer's mind relative to competitors. You don't position your product — you position it in someone's head. The actual product is secondary; the perception is what matters.
Week 4 · Branding

Identity, image and equity

Common trap
Brand Identity≠Brand Image
Identity = what the company intends to project. You control it: logo, colours, tone, messaging. Image = what the customer actually perceives. You don't control it. A brand can intend to look innovative (identity) but customers still see it as old-fashioned (image). Questions about "what the company sends out" = identity. "How customers see it" = image.
Not obvious
Brand equity pyramid
You build equity bottom-up, not top-down. You can't have loyalty without quality perception. You can't have quality perception without associations. You can't have associations without awareness. The exam might describe a company skipping steps — that's always a wrong strategy. Start at awareness, earn loyalty.
Week 5 · Internationalization

BCG vs Ansoff, and entry mode logic

Most confused pair in the course
BCG Matrix≠Ansoff Matrix
Both are 2×2 grids and both involve "markets" and "products" in some form. The difference: BCG looks at your existing product portfolio — it tells you what to fund, milk or drop based on market growth and your share. Ansoff looks at growth direction — it tells you where to expand next. BCG = "what do we do with what we have?" Ansoff = "where do we go next?"
Common trap
Market Development≠Product Development
Students flip these constantly. Market Development = existing product, new market (Harley taking its bikes to India). Product Development = new product, existing market (Harley launching the LiveWire electric bike to its current US fans). The thing that stays the same is in the name: market development = the product stays, the market is new. Product development = the market stays, the product is new.
Not obvious
StandardizationvsAdaptationvsGlocalization
Standardization = exact same product and marketing everywhere. Adaptation = change things per country. Glocalization = a strategy that does both at once: keep the core brand the same globally, but adapt local execution. It's not a middle ground — it's a deliberate combination. "Think global, act local."
Week 6 · Customer Experience Metrics

The metrics that get swapped on every exam

The most tested swap
NPS≠CSAT≠CES
All three measure how customers feel, but differently. NPS = loyalty — "would you recommend us?" (0–10 scale). CSAT = satisfaction with a specific interaction. CES = effort — "how hard was it to get this done?" The exam loves describing a scenario and asking which metric fits. "Customer recommends" → NPS. "Happy with the call" → CSAT. "How easy was it to resolve" → CES.
Not obvious
NPS passives don't count
NPS = % Promoters minus % Detractors. Passives (7–8) are completely ignored in the calculation. A company with 60% passives could still have an NPS of +40 if their promoters (60%) minus detractors (20%) = 40. Students often include passives in the maths. Don't.
Not obvious
Omnichannel≠Multichannel
Multichannel = present on many channels (app, store, website). Omnichannel = those channels are fully connected and seamless. A customer can start on the app, continue in store, and finish online — all with shared data and no friction. Having many channels does NOT make you omnichannel. The key word is seamless integration.
Common trap
Cross-sell≠Upsell
Cross-sell = offer a related, different product (phone case with a phone). Upsell = offer a better/pricier version of the same thing (128GB instead of 64GB). Both increase revenue but in different directions: sideways (cross) vs upward (up).
Week 7 · CRM

CRM types and the lead funnel

Common trap
OperationalvsAnalyticalvsCollaborative CRM
Operational = automates daily tasks (sending follow-up emails, logging calls). It does the work. Analytical = mines data for insights (finding churn patterns, identifying high-value segments). It thinks. Collaborative = shares customer info across teams and sometimes partners. It connects. The exam will describe what a company is doing — match the verb: automate → operational, analyse → analytical, share → collaborative.
Not obvious
Lead generation≠Lead acquisition
Lead generation = capturing interest (the spark): someone fills in a form, downloads a guide, clicks an ad. Lead acquisition = the full process from generating that interest all the way through to becoming a customer. Generation is one step inside acquisition. Acquisition is the whole funnel.
Week 8 · Products & Services

Product levels and service traps

Not obvious
CorevsActualvsAugmented
Students put warranty and delivery in the "actual" product. They belong to augmented. The actual product is the physical thing: features, design, quality, brand name. The augmented = everything wrapped around it: warranty, after-sales, delivery, installation. The core is the deepest layer — the real need being solved, not the object itself ("transport and status", not "a motorcycle").
Not obvious
Intangibility≠Inseparability
Intangibility = you can't touch or try a service before you buy it. Inseparability = the service is produced and consumed at the same time, by the same person. A haircut is intangible (you don't know exactly what you'll get) AND inseparable (the barber cuts while you're sitting there — you can't separate production from consumption). Two different problems, both features of services.
Week 9 · Pricing

Pricing strategies that get confused

Common trap
Price skimming≠Premium pricing
Premium pricing = permanently high price to signal quality. The price stays high forever. Skimming = start very high at launch to capture early adopters, then lower the price over time as demand from early adopters is exhausted. Skimming is a launch tactic. Premium is a permanent position. Harley's LiveWire uses skimming — the price drops as it reaches a broader market.
Not obvious
Elastic≠Inelastic
The naming is counterintuitive. Elastic demand stretches and shrinks with price — it's price-sensitive. A small price rise → big drop in sales. Inelastic demand barely moves — people buy anyway. The more essential or unique the product, the more inelastic. Petrol, insulin, cigarettes = inelastic. Luxury snacks = elastic. Don't let the word "elastic" make you think it's rigid.
Not obvious
Cost-basedvsValue-based pricing
Cost-based = start from what it costs you, add a margin. Simple, ignores the customer completely. Value-based = start from what the customer believes it's worth, price accordingly. For premium brands this means you can charge far above cost because the perceived value is high. Emirates doesn't price flights based on fuel cost alone — they price based on what luxury travellers will pay.
Week 10 · Promotion

Promotion mix and budget traps

Not obvious
Advertising≠PR
Both spread the message but differently. Advertising = you pay for the placement, you control every word. PR = you earn the coverage (a journalist writes about you, a review goes viral). PR is seen as more credible because it's not paid for — the audience knows advertising is biased. PR is harder to control but more trusted.
Common trap
Push≠Pull
Push = push the product through the channel to the customer (trade discounts, sales force selling to retailers). The product is pushed down the chain. Pull = create so much consumer demand that customers pull the product through the channel themselves (they go to the retailer and ask for it). Strong brands mostly use pull. New brands need push to get shelf space.
Not obvious
Unaided recallis stronger thanAided recall
Unaided = someone names your brand with no prompt. Aided = they recognise you when they see your name. Unaided is harder to achieve and much more valuable — it means you're top of mind without any trigger. A brand that achieves unaided recall has deeper loyalty and is harder to displace by competitors.
Week 11 · Distribution

Channel intensity and conflict

Common trap
IntensivevsSelectivevsExclusive
It's not just about how many outlets — the choice signals your brand position. A luxury brand using intensive distribution (available everywhere) would destroy its premium image. A mass product using exclusive distribution would starve itself of sales. The choice has to match the brand: premium = exclusive, mass = intensive. Selective sits in the middle for mid-tier products.
Not obvious
Vertical conflict≠Horizontal conflict
The naming is spatial. Vertical = conflict between different levels of the channel (producer argues with retailer). Think of the channel as a vertical chain — the friction is between floors. Horizontal = conflict between companies at the same level (two retailers of the same brand competing against each other). Same floor, side by side.
Week 12 · Sales

AIDA vs Journey, and B2B vs B2C

Common trap
AIDA≠Customer Journey
Both describe a progression but they're different models for different purposes. AIDA ends in Action (the purchase). It's a sales and communication model. The Customer Journey ends in Advocacy (telling others). It's a relationship model. AIDA stops at the sale. The journey continues past the sale into retention and referral. If a question is about structuring a sales message → AIDA. Long-term customer relationship → Journey.
Not obvious
Follow-up (step 7)
Most people remember the first 6 steps of personal selling and forget step 7: follow-up. It's not just a courtesy — it's strategically the most important for repeat business and referrals. A customer who feels looked after after the sale is far more likely to come back and recommend. Skipping follow-up is the most common real-world sales mistake.
Not obvious
B2B≠B2C selling
B2B is not just "selling to companies." It means: fewer but larger buyers, multiple decision-makers in one deal, longer sales cycles, and rational/ROI-driven decisions. B2C is emotional, fast, one decision-maker. The IKEA app being used by architects, developers and contractors in client meetings = B2B. A regular customer using it at home to try a sofa = B2C. Same app, completely different selling contexts.
Week 13 · Social Selling

Social selling ≠ social media marketing

Most confused pair in this week
Social Selling≠Social Media Marketing
Social Media Marketing = broadcasting brand content to audiences. One-to-many, focused on reach, visibility, engagement metrics. Social Selling = using social platforms to build one-to-one relationships with specific prospects and move them toward a sale. It's about individual conversations, not broadcasts. A brand posting Instagram ads = SMM. A sales rep sending a LinkedIn message after engaging with a prospect's posts = social selling.
Common trap
Ecommerce≠Social commerce
Ecommerce = buying and selling on a dedicated online store or marketplace (your website, Amazon, Bol). Social commerce = the entire transaction happens inside the social media app without leaving it. Instagram Shop where you tap a product and buy without opening a browser = social commerce. Being redirected to IKEA's website from Instagram = ecommerce, not social commerce.
Across all weeks · The deepest traps

Things that seem logical but aren't — universal exam traps

Universal trap
The "best/primary/directly" word trap
When the question says "which tool best", "which metric measures this directly", or "the primary goal" — two answers will be partially correct. The one with the tightest direct link wins. Example: "measure satisfaction directly" → CSAT wins over NPS even though NPS correlates with satisfaction. Directly is the deciding word.
Universal trap
Textbook-correct beats real-world-clever
The exam answers align with what the textbook says the concept is, not what would actually work in real life. If a question about Harley's pricing mentions "early adopters" and "launch" — the answer is skimming, even if value-based also makes sense. Always match the scenario to the framework as it's defined in the course, not as common sense might suggest.
Universal pattern
Eliminate before choosing
Two options are usually obviously wrong in every MC question. Eliminate those first. The real decision is almost always between 2 remaining options, not 4. This halves the cognitive load on every question — and since guessing is penalised, you only guess if you can get it down to 2.
